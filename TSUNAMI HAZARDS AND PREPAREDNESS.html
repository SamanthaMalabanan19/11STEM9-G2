— A tsunami is a phenomenon wherein enormous and strong waves that
are responsible for the surge in water sometimes reach the heights that
ranges from 10 - 100 ft. It usually travels with the speed of 20 - 30 meters
per hour (mph). Earthquakes, underwater landslides, and volcanic
eruptions are the most common events that trigger the emergence of
tsunamis. The effects starts from the disruption of transportation and
electricity, to the damage and potential loss of infastracture, up until the
worst case scenario of having human casualties and deaths. The most
common indicators of a tsunami is the unusual behavior (rise and roar) of
the ocean.

ORIGIN
🌊 The word "tsunami" is originally a Japanese word that has been on the news since a
powerful earthquake sent a wall of water into Northeastern Japan. But for more
than 100 years, "tsunami" has been used in the English language until the present.


TSUNAMI HISTORY IN JAPAN

🌊 Japan is one of the countries that has the longest list of recorded tsunamis in the
National Oceanic and Atmospheric Administration (NOAA) , with the first tsunami
dating back to year 684.

🌊 According to Susan Napier, who is a Japanese Studies professor, the tsunami
history has impacted the cultural work in Japan. "There is this very intense
relationship that the Japanese have with the sea," Napier says. "They're an island
country; fish is the main staple of their diet; all the great woodblocks print artists have
many, many pictures of the ocean."

EVACUATION AND MITIGATION PLAN

Communities that understand their tsunami risks are better prepared to protect the
public in the event of a tsunami. Protective measures include:

🌊 Planning and practicing for response to tsunamis
🌊 Warning the public about its possible impact
🌊 Establishing, marking, and publicizing evacuation routes
🌊 Educating the public (residents and visitors) about tsunamis and
tsunami safety before a tsunami strikes



Other ways to prepare for and mitigate the potential impacts of a tsunami emphasize
thoughtful land-use planning and building design in tsunami hazard zones
and include the following:

🌊 Improving evacuation routes
🌊 Building tsunami evacuation structures
🌊 Limiting new development in tsunami hazard zones
🌊 Designing, siting, and building structures to minimize tsunami damage
🌊 Adopting building codes that address tsunamis
🌊 Protecting and strengthening existing structures and infrastacture that if
damaged would negatively affect response and recovery
🌊 Moving important community assets and vulnerable population
out of tsunami hazard zones
🌊 Planning for post-tsunami recovery


RECOMMENDATIONS FOR THE DRRM PLAN

🌊 Technical Expertise
Engage qualified local or international experts, with experience in the local area,
to determine the tsunami risk to your project. Request their assistance in design,
implementation and maintenance planning to minimize tsunami threat.

🌊 Regulations
Check with local authorities to identify any local regulations concerning tsunami hazard and
impacts. Ensure that the project conforms to existing tsunami avoidance zone land use planning
regulations, flood regulations and any existing plans for warning and evacuation.

🌊 Local Impact
Consider the effect that the destruction or serious damage to buildings and infrastructure
associated with the planned project could have on the local population and environment.

🌊 Early Warning Signals
Early warning may be required to enable successful evacuation and to enable temporary
shut-down of the project if this could reduce consequential and compounding damage. Consider
the requirements for successful evacuation of the project location in case of a warning
(e.g., adequate transportation, evacuation routes, and safe refuges by undertakeing
evacuation planning and exercises).
